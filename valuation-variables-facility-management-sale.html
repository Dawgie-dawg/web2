The Valuation Variables That Matter Most in a Facilities Management Sale

Sophisticated buyers evaluate a specific set of variables that drive outcomes post-close. Understanding what those variables are is the foundation of any credible sale preparation.

When a facilities management business comes to market, buyers do not simply apply a sector multiple to trailing EBITDA and call it a valuation. The process is considerably more granular than that. Sophisticated acquirers — whether financial sponsors or strategic buyers — are evaluating a specific set of variables that they know from experience drive outcomes post-close. Understanding what those variables are, and how they interact, is the foundation of any credible sale preparation.

Revenue Quality Is Not the Same as Revenue Size

The first thing a buyer wants to understand is not how large the business is, but how durable its revenue is. In FM, that means examining the contract portfolio in detail: average contract length, renewal rates, notice periods, and the degree to which revenue is truly recurring versus project-based or discretionary.

A business generating $40 million in revenue under multi-year integrated FM contracts with blue-chip counterparties is a fundamentally different asset from one generating $40 million from a mix of short-cycle maintenance work and one-off project revenues — even if the EBITDA figures look similar at first glance. Buyers will pay a significant premium for the former and apply material discount to the latter.

Sellers should be prepared to present their revenue in a way that makes durability legible. That means contract-by-contract analysis, weighted average remaining term calculations, and a clear narrative around renewal history and pipeline.

Customer Concentration

Customer concentration is one of the most frequently cited risk factors in FM transactions, and one of the most negotiated in deal structuring. As a rule of thumb, a single customer representing more than 20 to 25 percent of revenue will attract scrutiny; above 30 percent, buyers will typically seek structural protections — earnout provisions, escrow arrangements, or representations around contract continuity — to mitigate the binary risk of that relationship not surviving the transition.

This does not mean that high-concentration businesses cannot be sold at attractive valuations. It means that the concentration needs to be addressed in the preparation process, either through genuine diversification in the years before a transaction or through careful deal structuring that allocates the risk appropriately between buyer and seller.

Margin Profile and Cost Structure Transparency

Buyers in FM transactions pay close attention to the relationship between gross margin and contract type. Labour-heavy soft services businesses typically operate at lower gross margins than technically complex hard services operations. Neither is inherently better, but buyers want to understand the margin dynamics in detail: which contracts are most profitable, which are underperforming, and what the realistic trajectory looks like.

Of particular importance is the treatment of overhead costs and the normalisation of EBITDA. FM businesses commonly have owner-related expenses, related-party transactions, or non-recurring costs that need to be carefully identified and explained. A well-prepared quality of earnings analysis, ideally prepared by a reputable accounting firm prior to going to market, removes significant friction from the due diligence process and gives buyers confidence in the numbers they are underwriting.

Workforce and Subcontractor Dependency

The workforce model of an FM business has become an increasingly important valuation variable. Businesses that rely heavily on subcontractors for service delivery are perceived as structurally more vulnerable than those with a stable employed workforce — subcontractor relationships are harder to control, less predictable on cost, and can present liability exposure in certain jurisdictions.

  • Employee turnover rates and retention of key supervisory and technical staff.
  • The proportion of work delivered by employed versus subcontracted labour.
  • Union arrangements and collective bargaining agreements, particularly for buyers with no prior experience managing unionised workforces.
  • Compliance with employment law, particularly around wage and hour regulations, which have been an area of elevated litigation risk in the sector.

Growth Credibility

In a PE-backed transaction, the entry valuation is only meaningful in the context of projected exit valuation, which depends on EBITDA growth over the hold period. Buyers are therefore not simply buying today's earnings — they are buying a business they believe can grow those earnings materially over three to five years.

The credibility of the growth story matters enormously. Organic growth in FM typically comes from three sources: contract expansion with existing clients, new contract wins, and geographic or service-line extension. Sellers should be able to articulate a clear and evidenced case for each of these pathways — and, where applicable, demonstrate a pipeline of opportunities that are genuinely in progress rather than theoretical.

Businesses that can demonstrate a consistent track record of winning new contracts, supported by a functioning business development capability that is not entirely dependent on the founding principal, will command the most credible growth multiples.

Getting the Variables Right Before Going to Market

The variables described above are not mysteries to be uncovered during due diligence — they are knowable in advance, and the degree to which a seller has addressed them prior to launching a process is one of the strongest predictors of transaction outcome. Preparation is not about presenting a business in the most flattering possible light; it is about ensuring that the value inherent in the business is properly understood and fairly reflected in the price.

That is the work that separates a good transaction from an exceptional one.
