Preparing a Facilities Business for Sale: A Principal's Checklist

The principals who achieve the best transaction outcomes are those who have prepared systematically. A practical checklist covering the areas that most consistently determine whether a sale achieves its potential.

The decision to sell a facilities business is rarely made on a single afternoon. It typically follows years of building something — a client base, a team, a reputation — and the consideration of what a transition would mean for all of those things. What is often underestimated is the preparation required to translate that decision into a transaction that reflects the full value of what has been built.

This checklist is not exhaustive, but it covers the areas that most consistently determine whether a sale process achieves its potential or falls short of it.

Financial Clarity

The foundation of any sale process is a set of financial statements that buyers can rely on. For FM businesses, this typically means three years of audited or reviewed accounts, a clear and defensible EBITDA figure, and a detailed schedule of addbacks — owner compensation above market rate, personal expenses run through the business, non-recurring costs — that allows buyers to understand normalised profitability.

Businesses with complex revenue recognition, intercompany transactions, or related-party arrangements should address these proactively. A quality of earnings analysis, commissioned from a reputable accounting firm prior to going to market, is one of the most effective investments a seller can make. It identifies issues before buyers do, gives the seller time to resolve or explain them, and materially accelerates the due diligence process once a transaction is underway.

  • Three years of financial statements, preferably reviewed or audited.
  • A detailed P&L by business unit or service line, if applicable.
  • A schedule of normalising adjustments with supporting documentation.
  • Current year management accounts and a forward-looking budget with supporting assumptions.
  • A clear working capital analysis — buyers will want to understand the normal working capital profile of the business.

Contract Portfolio Documentation

In FM transactions, the contract portfolio is the asset. Buyers will scrutinise every significant contract: its term, its pricing mechanism, its renewal provisions, its termination rights, and whether it is assignable without client consent. The last point is critical — many FM contracts contain change-of-control provisions that require client notification or consent on a sale of the business, and identifying and managing these well in advance of a transaction is essential.

In FM transactions, the contract portfolio is the asset — and buyers will scrutinise every significant contract in detail.

Sellers should prepare a comprehensive contract schedule that summarises the key commercial terms of each significant agreement, the renewal history, and any known risks or sensitivities. Where contracts are approaching expiry or renewal, having those conversations with clients before launching a sale process — or at minimum, understanding the likely outcome — removes a significant source of buyer uncertainty.

Management Structure and Succession

One of the most common value detractors in FM transactions is over-dependence on the founding principal. If the business cannot be credibly described as capable of operating at its current level of performance without the seller in an operational role, buyers will price that risk into their offers — either through lower valuations, earnout structures that tie a portion of consideration to post-close performance, or both.

The preparation work here is genuinely difficult, because it requires building organisational capability over time rather than in the weeks before a process launches. The principals who achieve the best transaction outcomes are typically those who have been thoughtful about succession for several years before a sale — who have hired capable operational managers, delegated meaningful authority, and ensured that client relationships are held at the institutional level rather than personally.

Operational and Compliance Readiness

FM businesses operate in a heavily regulated environment, and buyers will conduct thorough compliance due diligence. The areas of highest focus are typically employment law compliance — particularly wage and hour practices, worker classification, and union arrangements — health and safety performance, environmental obligations, and licensing and certification requirements for technical staff.

A seller who enters a process with unresolved compliance issues, outstanding HSE notices, or unclear worker classification practices will find these surfacing as price chips during due diligence. Addressing them before the process begins — or at minimum, understanding them well enough to explain them credibly — is far preferable to managing them under the pressure of a live transaction.

Narrative and Positioning

The final element of sale preparation is the one that is most often underestimated by first-time sellers: the narrative. Buyers are not simply buying a set of financial statements — they are buying a business they intend to grow, and they need to believe in the story of where that growth will come from.

A well-prepared seller can articulate, clearly and with evidence, what makes their business competitively differentiated, what the organic growth opportunity looks like, why the client base is loyal and sticky, and what the management team is capable of doing with the resources a new owner brings. This narrative — developed before going to market, tested with advisors, and delivered consistently across multiple buyer conversations — is one of the most powerful drivers of valuation outcome.

The businesses that achieve the best results in a sale process are not always the largest or the most profitable. They are the ones whose owners understood what buyers were looking for — and made sure the business was ready to demonstrate it.
