Structured Sale vs. Bilateral Negotiation: Which Process Is Right for Your Business?

The choice between a competitive sale process and a bilateral negotiation is one of the most consequential decisions in any transaction. Here's how to think about it.

One of the earliest and most consequential decisions in any M&A process is the choice of process structure. For owners of facilities management businesses, the question typically comes down to two broad approaches: a structured sale — in which the business is presented to a defined universe of buyers in a controlled, competitive process — or bilateral negotiation, in which the seller engages directly with a single counterparty, often one with whom there is an existing relationship or prior expression of interest.

Both approaches have genuine merit. The right choice depends on the specific characteristics of the business, the seller's priorities, and the nature of the buyer universe.

The Case for a Structured Sale

A structured sale process — sometimes referred to as a controlled auction or limited auction, depending on the breadth of outreach — is designed to create competitive tension. When multiple qualified buyers are evaluating the same opportunity simultaneously, under the same information conditions and on a defined timeline, the seller gains meaningful negotiating leverage.

The principal advantage is price discovery. No seller knows with certainty what the market will pay for their business until they test it. A well-run structured process reveals the full range of buyer appetite and allows the seller to transact with the party offering the best overall terms — not merely the highest headline price, but the combination of valuation, certainty of close, deal structure, and post-close considerations that best serves the seller's objectives.

Structured processes also tend to accelerate timelines. With a defined schedule of information release, management presentations, and bid deadlines, buyers are incentivised to move with focus and discipline. This reduces the risk of a process drifting or a buyer using extended exclusivity to renegotiate terms.

The Limitations of a Structured Sale

The disadvantages of a structured process are real and should not be dismissed. Most significantly, a broad competitive process involves a degree of market exposure that can be uncomfortable for owner-operators. Employees, customers, and competitors may become aware that the business is for sale, creating operational uncertainty and potentially affecting relationships that are core to the business's value.

Structured processes also require significant preparation. A compelling information memorandum, a well-organised data room, and a management team capable of presenting credibly to multiple buyers over a compressed period are prerequisites for a process that achieves its objectives. Businesses that go to market underprepared often find that the competitive tension they expected to generate never materialises.

The Case for Bilateral Negotiation

A bilateral approach — engaging directly with a single buyer, typically under a non-disclosure agreement and with some form of exclusivity arrangement — has real advantages in the right circumstances. The process is quieter, more controllable, and typically less disruptive to the ongoing operation of the business. It also allows for a deeper, more relationship-driven negotiation in which both parties can develop genuine alignment on structure, transition, and the future of the business.

Bilateral negotiations are often appropriate when the seller has a strong prior relationship with a specific buyer, when the business has characteristics that make it particularly valuable to one strategic acquirer, or when the seller has reasons to prioritise confidentiality and speed over maximum price.

  • The seller has received a compelling, unsolicited approach and wants to explore it efficiently.
  • The business operates in a niche where the universe of logical buyers is genuinely small.
  • The seller's primary objective is finding the right long-term home for the business, with price as a secondary consideration.
  • Operational disruption from a broader process carries significant risk to business value.

The Risk of Bilateral Negotiation

The principal risk of a bilateral approach is that the seller gives up price discovery. Without competitive tension, there is no external validation of whether the agreed price represents fair market value — and in practice, bilateral negotiations consistently produce lower outcomes than competitive processes for comparable businesses.

There is also an informational asymmetry problem. A sophisticated buyer who knows they are the only party at the table has significant incentive to move slowly, identify issues in due diligence that support price adjustments, and use the seller's desire for certainty as leverage. Sellers who enter bilateral negotiations without experienced advisory support often find that the final price bears little resemblance to the number that opened discussions.

A Third Path: Targeted Outreach

In practice, many FM transactions fall between these two poles. A targeted process — in which a curated shortlist of five to eight highly qualified buyers is approached simultaneously, with a competitive structure but without the breadth of a full auction — can capture much of the price discovery benefit of a structured sale while preserving a greater degree of confidentiality and control.

This approach is particularly well-suited to FM businesses in the lower middle market, where the relevant buyer universe is identifiable, the business has specific characteristics that appeal to a defined set of acquirers, and the seller wants meaningful competition without the complexity of a broad process.

The Right Answer Depends on Your Business

There is no universally correct answer to the process question. What matters is that the choice is made deliberately, with a clear understanding of the trade-offs, and with the benefit of advisors who have executed both types of transactions in the FM sector and can give an honest assessment of which approach is likely to produce the best outcome for a specific business at a specific moment in the market.

The process structure is one of the few variables in a sale that the seller controls entirely. It deserves careful thought.
